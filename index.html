01 / about

A short version, in plain English.

Most of what I make starts as something in my own life that bugs me until I fix it. I work in two halves — software and mechanical — but they come from the same place: notice a problem, get a little obsessed, and build my way out of it.

Take the mechanical stuff: wanting my Honda Civic to be more efficient got me fixated on aerodynamic drag. To actually test a fix, I built a wind tunnel from scratch — soldered the load cells, ran the fluid sims — and it ended up being the first demonstration of passive vortex propulsion in air, which I presented at an international conference.

On the software side, I was losing hours to TikTok. Instead of deleting it, I decided the feed wasn't the problem — what it fed me was. So I built a news app with the same addictive, swipe-through algorithm, except it strips the bias out of every story. That one became my first startup.

Bumpers — v1

Fusion 360
04

Bumpers — v2

— so I redesigned them. Wraparound geometry that's quicker to swap and shrugs off hits better. v1 → v2 was the loop the whole season ran on.

Fusion 360
05

Ground intake — one motor

Chained every roller to a single motor, so one drive spins the whole intake. Fewer motors means less weight, less wiring, and less current to budget.

Fusion 360
06

Intake — two degrees of freedom

The next intake gained a second axis of motion and 3D-printed TPU rollers — grippy enough to grab a game piece without crushing it.

Fusion 360
07

Dual-filament roller

Printed the rollers in two materials in one job: a rigid PLA core for the shaft, a soft TPU skin for traction. One part doing two jobs.

Fusion 360
08

Powered linear slides

A motorized extension braced by unpowered perpendicular slides — the cross-bracing kills the wobble when it's reached out under load.

Fusion 360
09

Dual-slide extension

Two-stage slides with a 180° servo-driven pivot on the intake at the tip — it can reach out and flip orientation at the end of travel.

Fusion 360
10

Intake + ramp, jointed

Modeled the full intake-to-ramp path as one jointed assembly, so I could play the motion in CAD and catch collisions before cutting any metal.

Fusion 360
